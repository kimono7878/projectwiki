About the Milky Way Galaxy

Overview

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy approximately 100,000 light-years across, containing 100-400 billion stars. It's about 13.6 billion years old. Our solar system resides in the Orion Arm, about 27,000 light-years from the galactic center.

Structure

It features a central bar, two major spiral arms (Perseus and Scutum-Centaurus), and several minor arms. The galaxy is warped at its edges, surrounded by a dark matter halo, and hosts a supermassive black hole named Sagittarius A* at its core.

Interesting Facts

The Milky Way is actively merging with smaller galaxies like the Canis Major Dwarf. Its disk contains vast clouds of gas and dust, fueling star formation, and its halo extends far beyond the visible structure.

Planets in Our Solar System

Mercury

Mercury, the smallest planet, is only 4,880 km in diameter and orbits the Sun in 88 days. Its surface, scarred by craters, endures temperatures from 427°C to -173°C due to its lack of atmosphere. Recent data suggests possible water ice in shadowed craters.

Venus

Venus, Earth's twin in size (12,104 km diameter), has a thick CO₂ atmosphere causing a runaway greenhouse effect, with surface temperatures reaching 465°C. Its retrograde rotation takes 243 Earth days, and volcanic activity shapes its landscape.

Earth

Earth, 12,742 km in diameter, is the only known planet supporting life, with 71% of its surface covered by oceans. Its atmosphere, rich in nitrogen and oxygen, supports diverse ecosystems. It orbits the Sun in 365.25 days and has a protective magnetic field.

Mars

Mars, the red planet (6,792 km diameter), has a thin CO₂ atmosphere and features like Olympus Mons, the solar system's largest volcano. Evidence of ancient rivers and lakes suggests past water. Its two moons, Phobos and Deimos, are small and irregular.

Jupiter

Jupiter, a gas giant (139,820 km diameter), is the largest planet, with a mass 318 times Earth's. Its Great Red Spot is a storm larger than Earth, persisting for centuries. Jupiter has 95 known moons, including Ganymede, larger than Mercury.

Saturn

Saturn, known for its stunning ring system, is a gas giant (116,460 km diameter) with a density less than water. Its rings are made of ice and rock particles. Saturn has 145 moons, with Titan being larger than Mercury and having a thick atmosphere.

Uranus

Uranus, an ice giant (50,724 km diameter), has a unique 98° axial tilt, causing extreme seasons lasting 21 years. Its faint rings and methane-rich atmosphere give it a pale blue color. It has 27 known moons, named after literary characters.

Neptune

Neptune, another ice giant (49,244 km diameter), is known for its deep blue color due to methane. It has the solar system's strongest winds, up to 2,400 km/h, and a dynamic atmosphere with the Great Dark Spot. It has 14 known moons, including Triton.

Earth's Moon

Overview

The Moon, 3,476 km in diameter, is Earth's only natural satellite, orbiting in 27.3 days. Its synchronous rotation means the same side always faces Earth, known as tidal locking.

Formation

Likely formed 4.5 billion years ago from debris after a Mars-sized body collided with Earth, creating a molten disk that coalesced into the Moon.

Interesting Facts

The Moon's surface has craters, maria (basaltic plains), and highlands. It experiences moonquakes and is slowly drifting away from Earth at 3.8 cm per year.

Stars in the Milky Way

Overview

The Milky Way contains 100-400 billion stars, with most being red dwarfs. Stars vary in size, from tiny neutron stars to supergiants hundreds of times the Sun's diameter.

Life Cycle

Stars form in nebulae, fuse hydrogen into helium in their cores, and evolve into red giants, supernovae, or white dwarfs depending on their mass.

Interesting Facts

About 90% of Milky Way stars are red dwarfs, which burn for tens of billions of years. The galaxy's spiral arms are active star-forming regions, rich with bright, young stars.
